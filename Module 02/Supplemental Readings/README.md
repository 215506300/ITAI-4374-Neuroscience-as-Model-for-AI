# Module 2 — Supplemental Readings

This folder contains two supplemental readings for Module 2.

---

## 1. Neuronal Shape and Neuronal Information Processing
**Source:** Hutchins, J., Aune, L., & Jessop, R. — *Introduction to Neuroscience* (UEN Pressbooks)

An introduction to how the physical structure of neurons relates to their function. Topics covered include:

- The three main neuron shapes (multipolar, bipolar, and unipolar) and where each is found in the nervous system
- How neurons receive, process, and transmit information — from dendrites through the cell body to the axon terminals
- The concept of the "decision point" (axon hillock) and the conditions for firing an action potential
- The structure of the chemical synapse and the steps of neurotransmitter release
- Cytoarchitectonics: how the layered and columnar organization of the neocortex reflects its information-processing function
- Case study: the Purkinje cell of the cerebellum as an example of form following function

---

## 2. The Algonauts Project 2021 Challenge: How the Human Brain Makes Sense of a World in Motion
**Source:** Cichy, R.M., Dwivedi, K., Lahner, B., et al. (2021)

An overview of a research challenge at the intersection of neuroscience and artificial intelligence. Topics covered include:

- The challenge goal: predicting whole-brain fMRI responses to over 1,000 short video clips of everyday events
- Dataset structure: training set (1,000 videos) and test set (102 videos) recorded from 10 human participants
- Two challenge tracks — the Mini Track (9 key visual cortex regions) and the Full Track (whole brain)
- The voxel-wise encoding model approach: extracting features from computer vision models and linearly mapping them to individual voxel responses
- The baseline model (AlexNet) and its predictive performance
- A discussion of open challenges as scientific instruments, and the relationship between prediction and explanation in cognitive neuroscience

---

*These readings support the lecture material for Module 2 and provide both foundational neuroscience background (neuronal structure and signaling) and a contemporary application (computational modeling of brain responses to visual stimuli).*
