# 2. Core Learning Themes

## 2.1 What is Neuroscience?

Neuroscience is defined as:

- **Textbook:** the scientific study of the nervous system  
- **Practical:** understanding how three pounds of tissue creates your entire experience of being alive  

Vital stats from the booklet:  
> “~86 billion neurons, ~100 trillion synapses, ~20 watts.”

### Subfields most relevant for AI:
- **Computational Neuroscience** — What algorithms does the brain run?  
- **Cognitive Neuroscience** — Memory, attention, language, decision‑making  

These subfields provide the computational principles that AI can borrow.

---

## 2.2 Why Study the Brain for AI?

Module 02 expands Module 01’s argument: AI and neuroscience diverged for decades, but AI has now hit limitations the brain does not have.

### The Limitations Gap
Brains excel at:
- Few‑shot learning  
- Energy efficiency  
- Common sense  
- Continuous lifelong learning  
- Knowing what they don’t know  

AI struggles with all of these.

The booklet states:  
> “Current AI can beat world champions at Go, yet a three‑year‑old outperforms it at learning new concepts from single examples.”

# 3. The Brain’s Greatest Magic Trick: Constructing Reality

This is the central concept of Module 02.

### Sensation vs. Perception
- **Sensation:** raw signals  
- **Perception:** the brain’s *interpretation* of those signals  

The booklet emphasizes:  
> “Your brain has never seen light. It sits in darkness and receives only electrical signals.”

From these signals, the brain constructs:
- Objects  
- Colors  
- Space  
- Time  
- Meaning  
- Your conscious experience  

This is the foundation of **world models**.

---

# 4. World Models: How the Brain Builds Reality

A *world model* is the brain’s internal simulation of the external world. It:

- Represents objects and relationships  
- Predicts what will happen next  
- Updates based on sensory input  
- Supports imagination and planning  
- Allows counterfactual reasoning  

### Why world models matter for AI:
- Pattern matching ≠ understanding  
- AI needs internal models to generalize  
- World models improve robustness and consistency  

---

# 5. Four Major Theories of World Modeling

Module 02 introduces four complementary frameworks:

---

## 5.1 Thousand Brains Theory (Jeff Hawkins)

Key ideas:
- The neocortex contains ~150,000 **cortical columns**  
- Each column learns a complete model of objects  
- Columns use **reference frames** (like coordinate systems)  
- Columns reach **distributed consensus** through voting  

Quote from the booklet:  
> “Every cortical column builds its own model of the world.”

Implications for AI:
- Distributed, redundant models  
- Robustness through consensus  
- Sensorimotor learning as core to intelligence  

---

## 5.2 Predictive Coding

The brain is a **prediction machine**.

- Higher areas send predictions downward  
- Lower areas send prediction errors upward  
- Only *unexpected* information is transmitted  

This makes the brain:
- Efficient  
- Adaptive  
- Fast at inference  

---

## 5.3 Free Energy Principle (Karl Friston)

A unifying theory:

- Organisms minimize the gap between predictions and reality  
- They can do this by:
  - Updating internal beliefs (perception)  
  - Acting on the world (action)  

This leads to **active inference** — perception and action as one process.

---

## 5.4 Bayesian Brain Hypothesis

The brain represents beliefs as probabilities and updates them using Bayes’ rule.

Example from the booklet:  
> “When hearing rustling, the brain evaluates if it’s wind or a snake based on context.”

This explains:
- Uncertainty handling  
- Priors  
- Why illusions work  

---

# 6. Global Workspace Theory

A theory of consciousness and information integration.

- The brain is like a theater  
- Working memory is the “stage”  
- Specialized modules are the “audience”  
- Attention is the “spotlight”  

This theory explains:
- Attention  
- Conscious access  
- Flexible information sharing

# 7. Neuromyths to Avoid

Module 02 debunks common misconceptions:

- “We only use 10% of our brain” — false  
- “Left brain = logical, right brain = creative” — false  
- “Learning styles” — no evidence  
- “Brain = computer” — misleading  

Why this matters for AI:
> “Accurate brain knowledge leads to better engineering decisions.”
