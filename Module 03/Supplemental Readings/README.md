# Module 3: Supplemental Readings
## Computational Neuroscience — Foundations, Methods, and Applications

This folder contains five readings that together trace the arc of computational neuroscience: from its historical and philosophical origins, through its mathematical and biophysical foundations, to its modern applications in psychiatry and artificial intelligence.

---

### 1. The First Computational Theory of Mind and Brain
**Piccinini, G.** | *Synthese*, 2004

A historical and philosophical reconstruction of McCulloch and Pitts's landmark 1943 paper, "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity." Piccinini argues this work introduced the first modern computational theory of mind by mapping logical inference onto idealized neural networks. The paper traces McCulloch's intellectual journey from psychiatric theory to formal logic, Pitts's mathematical contributions, and the lasting (and sometimes misunderstood) influence of their framework on AI, computability theory, and the mind–body problem.

**Key concepts:** McCulloch–Pitts neurons, all-or-none law, finite automata, Church–Turing thesis, computational theory of mind

---

### 2. The Hodgkin-Huxley Heritage: From Channels to Circuits
**Catterall, Raman, Robinson, Sejnowski & Paulsen** | *Journal of Neuroscience*, 2012

A symposium review marking 60 years of influence from Hodgkin and Huxley's 1952 model of the action potential. The paper demonstrates how the H-H framework operates across three levels: (1) molecular structure of voltage-gated ion channels, (2) cellular dynamics of excitability and spike timing, and (3) computational modeling of neural circuits. Topics include sodium channel inactivation mechanisms, Class 1/2 neuron dynamics, stochastic firing, phase-resetting, and the metabolic cost of spiking.

**Key concepts:** voltage-gated sodium channels, action potential, excitability classes, phase-resetting curves, energy efficiency, computational modeling

---

### 3. Computational Neuroscience: A Frontier of the 21st Century
**Wang, Hu, Huang et al.** | *National Science Review*, 2020

A perspective piece making the case for computational neuroscience as a central pillar of 21st-century brain research, with particular attention to the emerging Chinese neuroscience landscape. The authors outline three modeling approaches (descriptive, normative, mechanistic), discuss the growing dialogue between computational neuroscience and AI, and argue for coordinated infrastructure—centers, summer schools, and collaborative funding programs—to advance the field.

**Key concepts:** descriptive/normative/mechanistic models, brain connectomics, cognitive functions, computational psychiatry, brain-inspired AI, China Brain Project

---

### 4. Computational Neuroscience Approach to Psychiatry: Theory-Driven Approaches
**Khaleghi, Mohammadi, Shahi & Nasrabadi** | *Clinical Psychopharmacology and Neuroscience*, 2022

A review of theory-driven computational models in psychiatry, organized around three model types: **phenotypic** (simulating observable symptoms), **neurobiological** (modeling low-level neural mechanisms), and **intermediate** (bridging the two). The intermediate category receives the most focus and is subdivided into synthetic, algorithmic reinforcement learning, and Bayesian models. The paper includes applications to schizophrenia, depression, ADHD, autism, and addiction, and discusses how theory- and data-driven approaches can be productively combined.

**Key concepts:** computational psychiatry, phenotypic/neurobiological/intermediate models, reinforcement learning, Bayesian inference, dopamine, schizophrenia, precision psychiatry

---

### 5. We First Need to Understand How the Brain Works If We Want True AI
**MIT Technology Review**, 2021 — Interview with Jeff Hawkins

A practitioner's perspective on the gap between current AI and genuine machine intelligence, drawing on Hawkins's "Thousand Brains" theory of the neocortex. Hawkins argues that AI has stalled because it ignores key principles of biological intelligence: learning through movement, parallel cortical columns that "vote" on percepts, continuous learning without catastrophic forgetting, and knowledge organized around reference frames. The piece raises provocative questions about embodiment, benchmarks, and the long-term purpose of building intelligent machines.

**Key concepts:** thousand brains theory, cortical columns, embodiment, continuous learning, reference frames, neocortex, neuroscience-inspired AI

## Reading Suggestions

These readings work well in the following order:

1. **Piccinini** — establishes the historical and philosophical foundation; why did people start thinking of the brain as a computing device?
2. **Catterall et al.** — grounds the field in rigorous biophysics; what does it actually mean to model a neuron?
3. **Wang et al.** — zooms out to the field as a whole; what are the modeling strategies and where is the field headed?
4. **Khaleghi et al.** — applies these tools to a clinical domain; how do computational models help us understand mental illness?
5. **Hawkins (MIT TR)** — returns to the big picture; what does understanding the brain mean for the future of AI?

---

## Thematic Threads

**Brain as computer** — Runs from McCulloch & Pitts (1943) through to Hawkins's critique of shallow analogies in modern deep learning.

**Levels of analysis** — All five readings, in different ways, grapple with the challenge of relating molecular/cellular mechanisms to behavior and cognition.

**Modeling philosophy** — The tension between biological realism and mathematical tractability appears in Piccinini (H-H as exemplar), Wang et al. (three model types), and Khaleghi et al. (phenotypic vs. neurobiological vs. intermediate).

**Clinical translation** — Khaleghi et al. is the primary reading here, but Wang et al. also addresses computational psychiatry as a key application area.
