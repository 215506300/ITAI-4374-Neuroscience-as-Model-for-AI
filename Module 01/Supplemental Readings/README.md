# 1. Salvatori et al. (2025) — *Brain‑Inspired Computational Intelligence via Predictive Coding*

## Purpose of the Paper
This paper surveys **predictive coding (PC)** as a biologically grounded alternative to backpropagation.  
It explains why PC is attractive for next‑generation AI: locality, robustness, energy efficiency, and biological plausibility.

## Why It Matters for Module 1
Module 1 argues that AI must move beyond dense, feedforward, static architectures.  
Predictive coding provides a **mechanistic example** of how the brain performs learning and inference using:

- **Local credit assignment**  
- **Sparse, energy‑efficient computation**  
- **Continuous updating of beliefs**  
- **Generative modeling instead of pure discrimination**

The paper states:  
> “Modern deep learning models are notoriously computationally expensive, untrustable, and biologically implausible.”  

This directly reinforces Module 1’s critique of current AI assumptions.

## Key Takeaways
- PC replaces backprop with **local prediction‑error minimization**.  
- PC networks can train **cyclic, recurrent, and entangled architectures**—unlike backprop.  
- PC is mathematically grounded in **variational free energy minimization**.  
- PC unifies perception, learning, and inference under one framework.

# 2. Zador et al. (2023) — *Catalyzing Next‑Generation AI through NeuroAI*

## Purpose of the Paper
This perspective argues that **neuroscience must guide the next era of AI**, especially for embodied, sensorimotor, and adaptive intelligence.

## Why It Matters for Module 1
Module 1 emphasizes that intelligence is **not just benchmarks**—it’s real‑world, adaptive, continuous behavior.  
This paper expands that idea into a concrete proposal: the **Embodied Turing Test**.

The authors write:  
> “Today’s AI systems cannot compete with the sensorimotor capabilities of a four‑year‑old child or even simple animals.”  

This supports Module 1’s point that AI lacks robustness, flexibility, and real‑world grounding.

## Key Takeaways
- Proposes the **Embodied Turing Test** as a new benchmark for AI.  
- Argues that **animal‑level intelligence** (navigation, manipulation, adaptation) is the real foundation of general intelligence.  
- Highlights the brain’s **energy efficiency**, **recurrence**, and **robustness** as essential design principles.  
- Calls for a large‑scale NeuroAI research program.

# 3. Fan et al. (2023) — *Towards NeuroAI: Introducing Neuronal Diversity into Artificial Neural Networks*

## Purpose of the Paper
This paper examines **neuronal diversity** in the brain and argues that artificial networks should incorporate diverse neuron types to improve efficiency, robustness, and memory.

## Why It Matters for Module 1
Module 1 stresses that AI systems rely on **dense, homogeneous computation**, while the brain uses **sparse, diverse, specialized neurons**.

The paper states:  
> “The brain has numerous morphologically and functionally different neurons, while artificial neural networks are almost exclusively built on a single neuron type.”  

This directly supports Module 1’s discussion of sparse computation and biological constraints.

## Key Takeaways
- Biological neurons differ in morphology, function, firing patterns, and computational roles.  
- Diversity enables:  
  - **Energy efficiency**  
  - **Interpretability**  
  - **Resistance to catastrophic forgetting**  
  - **Robustness to noise and perturbations**  
- Artificial networks may benefit from neuron types inspired by dendritic computation, spiking behavior, and nonlinear ion‑channel dynamics.
